Detailed Explanation

Domain-01 exists because the dental tourism market is structurally asymmetric. A Costa Rica clinic knows exactly what procedures cost, which upsells generate margin, and what a North American patient with no clinical reference documents will accept. You enter that market with verified clinical data or you enter it as an uninformed buyer. This chapter is the instrument that reverses the information asymmetry before it has any financial consequence.

The CBCT scan is not optional when implants are planned. A panoramic X-ray is a two-dimensional projection that shows tooth positions and gross pathology but cannot measure bone height, bone density, or the three-dimensional proximity of the inferior alveolar nerve or maxillary sinus — all of which determine whether a standard implant can be placed or whether bone grafting, a sinus lift, or a shorter implant body is required. A Costa Rica implantologist who receives only a panoramic image must either proceed with inadequate site data or order a repeat CBCT. The repeat scan costs you money and time. The insufficient site data costs you a failed implant. Neither outcome is acceptable when both are preventable by a single imaging request at the domestic appointment.

The CDT code mapping discipline introduced in this chapter is not bureaucratic formalism — it is the mechanism by which you detect scope creep in real time. When a Costa Rica clinic adds 'bone augmentation procedure' to a proposal line item, that maps to D4263 or D4264 in CDT nomenclature. Your domestic plan either includes that code or it does not. If it does not, the procedure is either genuinely indicated by new clinical findings the Costa Rica dentist has observed, or it is a revenue-generating addition. You cannot tell which without the CDT baseline. With it, you send the line item back to your domestic dentist for a 48-hour remote review and receive a clinical opinion before paying for anything.

The $15,000 second-opinion threshold is calibrated to the inflection point at which clinician-discretionary procedures statistically appear in domestic treatment plans. Below $15,000, most treatment plans represent urgent or clearly necessary restorative work. At and above $15,000, the plan increasingly includes procedures where reasonable clinicians disagree — prophylactic extractions of teeth showing early periapical changes, full-arch crown replacement of teeth with marginal restorations that are not yet failing, and bone grafting scopes that exceed what the CBCT actually demonstrates is deficient. A second opinion at this threshold is not a sign of distrust toward your domestic dentist. Detailed Explanation

The architecture of this chapter rests on a single operational principle: confirmed bookings must be structurally incapable of forcing a clinical compromise. This is not a preference — it is a risk control mechanism. The moment a fixed departure date enters the system, it becomes a variable that the treating dentist implicitly factors into discharge timing. That implicit pressure is invisible, undocumented, and functionally unavoidable when the dentist knows the patient boards a plane in 18 hours.

Flexible fare selection is the most common area where patients underestimate operational risk. The language 'free change' appears on fares across major carriers but frequently conceals a fare-differential charge that becomes material when the patient needs to extend by two days during peak travel periods. The only operationally sound approach is to read the specific policy language before purchase — not the marketing summary, but the tariff terms — and verify that date changes carry no fee of any kind up to at least 14 days from the original departure. If that language does not exist in the fare rules, the fare is disqualified regardless of price.

Accommodation vetting through WS-04-B addresses a category of failure that patients consistently underestimate until they experience it. Ground-floor access on a sedation day is not a comfort preference — it is a fall-risk mitigation for a patient whose coordination and judgment are clinically impaired for 4–8 hours post-procedure. Kitchen access is not a budget preference — it is the only reliable mechanism for executing a 5-day soft-food recovery protocol when local restaurant soft-food options are limited, unfamiliar, and expensive. Pharmacy proximity is not a convenience preference — it is the difference between a 15-minute antibiotic fill and a 90-minute taxi round trip for a patient with a 101°F fever at 9:00 PM.

The emergency contact registry in WS-04-C operates as a cognitive load reduction tool during the highest-stress scenario this system addresses: an active post-surgical complication in a foreign country. The registry must be printed — not saved to a phone that may be dead, locked, or inaccessible — and placed in a visible location in the accommodation before the first appointment. Detailed Explanation

The structural risk that this chapter is designed to neutralize is not unique to Costa Rica — it is the universal dynamic of any high-cost service transaction where the buyer pays most of the money before the work is done. Dental tourism amplifies this risk because the buyer is physically located 2,500 miles from the service provider, the service is irreversible, and the buyer typically lacks the clinical vocabulary to evaluate whether what was delivered matched what was contracted. Orientation Snapshot

You are back home. The surgical phase is done. What happens in the next six months determines whether your implants become permanent, load-bearing restorations or become explantation cases. This chapter governs that interval — from the 14-day domestic check-in through the 6-month radiographic osseointegration confirmation that authorizes Trip 2 and closes the clinical case.

The stakes are concrete. Early peri-implantitis detected at 3 weeks is reversible with targeted antibiotic therapy. The same condition detected at 16 weeks has typically progressed to measurable crestal bone loss, jeopardizing osseointegration and potentially requiring explantation at $2,500–$4,000 per site. This chapter assigns specific radiograph types to specific intervals, gives you exact escalation triggers, and defines the documentary requirements for warranty claims against the Costa Rica clinic. Every section is executable. Nothing here is observational.

Decision Guide
14-Day Appointment Finding: Normal vs. Acute
If: Soft tissue shows no swelling beyond expected healing, no suppuration, no exposed membrane, and the provisional is intact with normal occlusal contacts
Then: Log findings in ws-06-a, schedule the 6-week periapical radiograph before leaving the office, and enter the date into the Osseointegration Monitoring Schedule
Else: If any of the following are present — acute swelling, purulent discharge, exposed membrane, provisional fracture with implant site pain — immediately initiate the complication escalation protocol: document clinical findings with photos and written description, and notify the Costa Rica clinic in writing via ws-06-c within 72 hours
Escalate when: Fever above 101°F persisting more than 24 hours, any implant mobility detected on clinical exam, or purulent discharge from the implant site at any post-surgical point — these require same-day domestic dental evaluation in addition to cross-border notification
6-Week Periapical: Integration on Track vs. Early Warning
If: Periapical shows no radiolucent halo around the implant body and implant position is stable relative to surgical placement records
Then: Log findings in ws-06-a, note the periapical as a clean baseline, and schedule the 3-month periapical before leaving the office
Else: If a radiolucent zone is visible around any portion of the implant body, or if the domestic dentist notes crestal bone loss exceeding 1.0mm from platform at 6 weeks, activate ws-06-c and notify the Costa Rica clinic within 72 hours with the radiograph image attached
Escalate when: Any visible radiolucent halo at 6 weeks is an immediate escalation trigger — early failure at this interval is recoverable with prompt intervention but requires the Costa Rica clinic's written guidance on the management protocol within 10 business days of notification
3-Month Periapical: Stable Crestal Bone vs. Failure Indicator
If: Crestal bone levels are within 1.5mm of the implant platform, no saucer-shaped or angular bone defect is visible, and no probing depths above 5mm are reported
Then: Log findings in ws-06-a as a stable 3-month checkpoint, schedule the 4–6 month CBCT or full periapical series, and confirm the appointment date with the domestic dentist before leaving
Else: If crestal bone loss exceeds 1.5mm from platform at 3 months, initiate ws-06-c immediately; if peri-implant pocket depths exceed 5mm with patient-reported bleeding on brushing, the Costa Rica clinic must be notified in writing within 72 hours and must provide a management protocol within 10 business days
Escalate when: Bone loss exceeding 2.0mm from platform at 3 months combined with a saucer-shaped crestal defect visible on periapical is a peri-implantitis indicator requiring both domestic clinical evaluation and a written demand for Costa Rica clinic response within 10 business days
4–6 Month Final Imaging: Trip 2 Authorization vs. Interval Extension
If: CBCT or full periapical series shows no radiolucent halo, crestal bone loss within 1.0–1.5mm of platform, no thread exposure beyond initial healing margin, and no peri-implant pocket depths above 4mm — confirmed in writing by the domestic monitoring dentist
Then: Transmit the imaging report and the domestic dentist's written clearance to the Costa Rica lead dentist; obtain the lead dentist's written confirmation of readiness for abutment placement and crown delivery; activate domain-03 Trip 2 trigger and proceed to domain-04 flight booking under the flexible-fare rule
Else: If integration is incomplete or equivocal on any implant site, extend the osseointegration interval by a minimum of 6–8 weeks, enter the new imaging date in ws-06-a with the clinical rationale documented, and do not initiate any flight or accommodation booking until the extended imaging is complete and both dentists have issued written clearance
Escalate when: Any confirmed radiolucent halo at the final imaging interval, or mobility on clinical percussion testing, requires immediate ws-06-c notification to the Costa Rica clinic — this is a potential osseointegration failure requiring the clinic's written protocol for explantation planning or salvage intervention
Provisional Fracture: Monitor vs. Escalate
If: Provisional fractures or becomes dislodged but the implant site is fully submerged, overlying tissue shows no disruption, and the patient reports no localized pain at the implant site following the fracture event
Then: Replace the provisional with a domestic dentist, document the replacement with date and clinical notes, notify the Costa Rica clinic of the provisional failure and domestic repair in writing as an informational update (not a warranty claim), and continue the monitoring schedule without interruption
Else: If the fracture event caused direct pressure on the implant platform, or if the patient reports localized pain at the implant site following the fracture, obtain a periapical radiograph within 5 business days and activate ws-06-c if any radiographic change from the prior baseline is detected
Escalate when: Visible soft tissue migration over the implant platform following provisional loss requires same-day domestic dental evaluation and immediate written notification to the Costa Rica clinic — migration can obstruct abutment placement and alter the crown delivery timeline

Detailed Explanation

The post-return interval is the phase of dental tourism most underestimated by patients who feel well after surgery. The absence of pain is not a clinical signal. Osseointegration is a biological process that occurs below the threshold of perception, and the conditions that threaten it — early peri-implantitis, crestal bone resorption, graft membrane exposure — are radiographically detectable weeks before they produce symptoms the patient can feel. This chapter is designed around that asymmetry: the monitoring schedule is mandatory precisely because it operates independently of how the patient feels.

The radiograph sequencing protocol is the operational core of this chapter. Three imaging intervals serve three distinct clinical purposes. The 6-week periapical establishes the earliest post-placement baseline — it confirms implant position stability and the absence of a radiolucent halo, which would indicate early osseointegration failure. This image also becomes the comparative reference for the 3-month periapical. Without the 6-week baseline, the 3-month image cannot be meaningfully interpreted for crestal bone change because there is no prior reference point. Patients and domestic dentists who skip the 6-week imaging and go directly to a 3-month radiograph have inadvertently destroyed their ability to measure whether crestal bone loss is new or pre-existing — a distinction the Costa Rica clinic will rely on to accept or deny a warranty claim.

The 3-month periapical is the early warning gate. Crestal bone loss exceeding 1.5mm at 3 months is a failure indicator, not a borderline finding. At this interval, peri-implantitis that is detectable on a periapical has already progressed beyond the subclinical stage. The clinical question at 3 months is not whether the implant looks acceptable — it is whether the bone loss trajectory, if extrapolated to 6 months, will still fall within osseointegration success criteria or will have crossed into failure territory. The escalation threshold is set at 1.5mm precisely because it provides a remediation window: targeted antibiotic therapy and a modified oral hygiene protocol initiated at 3 months can arrest progression before the 6-month confirmation imaging.

The 4–6 month CBCT or full periapical series is the authorization gate for Trip 2. It is not a formality. The specific criteria — no radiolucent halo, crestal bone loss within 1.0–1.5mm of platform, no thread exposure beyond initial healing margin, no peri-implant pocket depths above 4mm — are the same criteria the Costa Rica treating dentist will apply in-clinic before proceeding with abutment placement.
